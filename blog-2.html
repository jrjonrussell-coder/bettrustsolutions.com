March 2026 — Regulation & Safer Gambling

Liberalisation, Channelization, and the Athlete

By Jonathan Russell — Sports Betting & Integrity Consultant

The regulated industry’s central argument for market liberalisation has never been that betting is inherently harmless. It has been something more precise and more defensible: that betting markets exist regardless of legal status, and that regulation is the only mechanism capable of imposing consumer protections, integrity frameworks, and accountability on those markets. Prohibition does not eliminate demand. It relocates it to environments where none of the safeguards that regulated operators are obligated to maintain can function.

This is not ideology. It is an empirically supported position. The experience of markets that have transitioned from prohibition or monopoly to regulated competition — Sweden, Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, and more recently Brazil and Ontario — consistently demonstrates that channelization into licensed frameworks increases suspicious betting reporting, improves AML compliance, reduces unlicensed operator activity, and enables mandatory responsible gambling interventions. These outcomes are not hypothetical. They are measurable consequences of the regulatory architecture that liberalisation creates.

The channelization argument is not that all forms of gambling are equivalent. It is that the regulated market, with all its imperfections, is structurally superior to its illegal alternative in almost every dimension that matters.

The industry’s credibility rests on this argument remaining grounded in evidence rather than commercial interest. When it is defended on the basis of consumer choice or economic contribution alone, it becomes vulnerable. When it is defended on the basis of harm reduction, integrity infrastructure, and accountable governance, it is on considerably stronger ground.

What the Evidence on Athlete Welfare Actually Shows

The regulated industry must engage honestly with what has emerged from the research. It cannot be dismissed as regulatory overreach or moral panic.

The NCAA’s 2025 quantitative surveys — covering over 20,000 student-athletes across 493 institutions — documented that 46% of Division I men’s basketball athletes received negative or threatening messages traceable to individuals who had wagered on their games. Over a quarter reported verbal or physical incidents. Among professional athletes, an anonymous multi-league survey found 78% of MLB players reported that the expansion of legal sports betting had materially changed how fans treated them. These are not marginal figures.

The specific mechanism at issue is the player proposition bet: a market structured not around the outcome of a competition but around an individual’s performance. The granularity of these markets — whether a specific player will record more or fewer than a defined number of rushing yards, passing completions, or three-point attempts — creates a direct and traceable line between a bettor’s financial outcome and a named individual’s physical performance. That is a qualitatively different exposure from betting on a match result.

The industry cannot coherently argue that the evidence of athlete harm is fabricated by those opposed to gambling liberalisation. The data has been produced by credible institutions using robust sample sizes. The appropriate response is engagement, not dismissal.

The Distinction the Industry Must Make Explicit

The central strategic error the regulated industry could make in this debate is to treat all prop bet restrictions as equivalent threats to the channelization argument. They are not. The welfare case against player prop bets falls along a clear gradient, and the industry’s response should be calibrated accordingly.

The case against player prop betting on college and amateur athletes is, on the available evidence, compelling. These are individuals operating without professional player union representation, without legal counsel embedded in their institutional relationships, without the financial resources to absorb reputational damage from public blame, and in many cases without the psychological infrastructure to manage the targeted abuse that follows a bad performance that cost bettors money. The proposition that a regulated market should offer granular performance markets on these athletes is difficult to defend on channelization grounds, because the harm being caused is not being eliminated by the licensed framework — it is being enabled by it.

The case against player prop betting on professional athletes is materially different. Professional athletes operate within collective bargaining frameworks. Their unions — the MLBPA, the NFLPA, the NBPA, and their equivalents in European football — have the institutional capacity to engage with regulators and operators on the conditions under which performance markets are offered. The harassment concern is real, but it is addressable through operator-level conduct frameworks, platform moderation obligations, and fan behaviour codes, rather than market prohibition.

The regulated industry’s position should be differentiated, not uniform. Concede what cannot be defended. Defend vigorously what can be. Amateur athlete prop markets fall into the first category. Professional athlete markets, appropriately governed, fall into the second.

The Credibility Problem and How to Avoid It

There is a structural risk in the industry’s current posture. When commercial operators lobby against all prop bet restrictions without making the amateur/professional distinction, they reinforce the perception that their arguments for liberalisation are a function of revenue protection rather than genuine harm reduction commitment. That perception is corrosive to the channelization thesis, because it invites the inference that the industry will defend any profitable product category regardless of evidence.

The more strategically and ethically coherent position is this: the regulated industry proactively concedes the indefensible, thereby strengthening its position on everything else. Voluntary withdrawal from college athlete prop markets — or active support for the regulatory bans that 18 US states have already enacted — would constitute a material demonstration that the industry’s harm reduction commitment is genuine. It would also reduce the legislative surface area available to prohibitionist advocates seeking broader restrictions.

This is not a novel concept in regulated industries. Pharmaceutical companies routinely withdraw products from specific patient populations where evidence of harm is disproportionate, while defending those products’ availability for populations where the benefit-to-risk calculation is different. That is responsible product stewardship, not commercial weakness. The iGaming industry is capable of the same logic.

What the Industry Should Advocate For

Player unions should be active participants in the product design process for performance markets — not passive subjects of decisions made by operators and regulators. The MLBPA and NFLPA have already indicated interest in this kind of engagement. The industry should actively pursue it, because a jointly designed framework is considerably more defensible than one imposed over athlete objection.

Operators should implement platform-level conduct frameworks that treat betting-related harassment as a terms-of-service violation, with meaningful enforcement. Several major platforms have begun this work, but it remains inconsistent. Regulators should be lobbied to mandate minimum standards across all licensees rather than allowing it to remain voluntary best practice.

The regulatory regimes that mandate IBIA membership — most clearly the UK, and operationally several European markets — have demonstrated that integrity monitoring infrastructure functions more effectively inside the regulated market than outside it. Extending that logic to a formalised athlete welfare monitoring framework, in which operators report and respond to bettor-athlete harassment patterns, is a natural and defensible extension of the integrity architecture already in place.

Finally, the industry should commission the quantitative research that does not yet exist: large-scale surveys of professional athletes’ attitudes toward performance markets on their own activities. The absence of this data leaves the debate structurally asymmetric — the NCAA’s data on college athletes is being used to fill the evidentiary gap for professional sport, and that inference is being made by parties who are not sympathetic to the industry’s position. The industry has the resources and the incentive to generate more precise evidence. Not generating it is a choice, and it is not a well-reasoned one.

Conclusion

The channelization argument is correct. Regulated markets are demonstrably superior to their illegal alternatives across the dimensions that matter for public policy: consumer protection, integrity monitoring, AML compliance, and problem gambling intervention. This argument should be defended with precision and confidence.

But it should be defended precisely, not comprehensively. The regulated industry’s credibility on channelization depends on its willingness to acknowledge the product categories where regulated availability creates net harm. Player proposition betting on college and amateur athletes appears, on the current evidence, to be one of those categories. The sustainable industry position is not to resist that conclusion but to lead it — and to use the credibility that comes from genuine concession to defend the larger and more defensible case for liberalisation.

The industry that is seen to take athlete welfare seriously is far better positioned to resist prohibitionist pressure on the broader market. The industry that is seen to defend every revenue line regardless of evidence is not.

The difference between those two positions is a choice, and it is one the regulated industry has not yet made with sufficient clarity.
